Survival Analysis

Question: How long until an event occurs?Output: Survival curve + hazard ratiosDataset: survival.csv (800 rows)Use case: Customer lifetime modelling · Churn timing · Equipment failure · Loan default timing

Pipeline walkthrough — analyst workflow steps, pre-populated outputs, and business interpretation at each stage.

Stage 01

Time-to-Event Problem Setup

Survival analysis models the time until an event (churn, default, equipment failure) rather than whether the event happens. It correctly handles censored observations — customers who have not yet churned when you run the analysis.

  • Understand censoring A customer who joined 6 months ago and has not yet churned is censored: you know they survived at least 6 months, but you don't know when they'll churn. Standard classification ignores this information — survival analysis uses it correctly.Using logistic regression on tenure-restricted data without handling censoring underestimates churn risk for newer cohorts.
  • Set up the survival dataset Two key columns: T = time to event (or time to censoring), E = event indicator (1 = churned, 0 = censored/still active). Every customer needs both.
  • Choose the survival model Kaplan-Meier: non-parametric, produces survival curves without assumptions. Cox PH: semi-parametric, produces hazard ratios for features (like logistic regression coefficients). Accelerated Failure Time: parametric, directly models time-to-event.

Stage 02

Kaplan-Meier Survival Curve

The Kaplan-Meier curve answers: 'What fraction of customers survive to each time point?' It is the primary descriptive tool before any modelling.

  • Fit KaplanMeierFitter on the full dataset Produces a step function: probability of survival (P(T > t)) at each observed event time.
  • Plot survival curve with confidence intervals X-axis = months. Y-axis = probability of survival (not yet churned). Confidence interval widens over time as fewer customers remain at risk.
  • Stratify by key variables Fit separate KM curves for high vs low tenure cohorts, Gold vs Silver tier, or phone vs digital channel. Log-rank test determines whether curves are statistically different.

What this tells you
  • The survival curve shows the churn problem is front-loaded: 17% churn in the first year, but only 9% more in years 2-3. Early-life retention is the highest-ROI investment.
  • Gold tier's survival advantage is significant (p=0.0003) and large (+27 months median survival). Tier upgrade programmes have a measurable impact on customer lifetime.
  • Median survival of 62 months means the expected customer lifetime is ~5 years — this is the denominator for CLTV calculations.
Survival analysis ≠ hazard at a single time pointA logistic regression churn model tells you P(churn | alive at month 12). Survival analysis tells you the full time profile. For cohort-level revenue planning, you need the full curve — a single-point classifier loses the timing information that drives cash flow modelling.
Stage 03

Cox Proportional Hazards Model

The Cox model estimates the effect of features on the hazard rate — how much each feature accelerates or decelerates the time to churn. Output is hazard ratios, analogous to odds ratios in logistic regression.

  • Fit CoxPHFitter with key covariates Fit on features: tenure_months, support_calls, logins_per_week, cltv, tier, channel. Model estimates the hazard ratio for each feature.Hazard ratio interpretation: HR > 1 = increases churn rate. HR < 1 = decreases churn rate. HR = 0.6 means 40% lower hazard of churning at any point in time.
  • Check the proportional hazards assumption Cox PH assumes that the hazard ratio between groups is constant over time. Test with Schoenfeld residuals. If violated, use time-varying coefficients or stratify the model.
  • Compare to logistic regression coefficients Cox HRs and LR ORs should point in the same direction. Disagreement suggests a violation of assumptions or data quality issue in one model.

Output: QA Checklist Checklist
Good
  • Censoring correctly handled (E=0 for active customers)
  • KM curve plotted before Cox model
  • PH assumption tested with Schoenfeld residuals
  • Cox HRs consistent in direction with logistic regression ORs
  • Median survival time reported as key business metric
Red flags
  • Logistic regression used on survival data without handling censoring
  • PH assumption not tested
  • Model reported only on subset of customers with complete churn (ignores censored observations entirely)
  • Hazard ratios interpreted as probabilities (they are relative rates, not probabilities)
