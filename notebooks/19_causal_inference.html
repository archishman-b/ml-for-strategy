Stage 01

The Fundamental Distinction: Correlation vs Causation

Causal inference asks: 'What would have happened if we had done something different?' This counterfactual question cannot be answered with standard regression alone — it requires either experimental design or careful observational techniques.

  • Understand the counterfactual problem We observe either the treatment or control outcome for each unit — never both. The 'potential outcome' we do not observe is the counterfactual. Estimating it is the core challenge of causal inference.Correlation tells you that A and B co-occur. Causation tells you that changing A produces a change in B. These are genuinely different — and strategy decisions require the latter.
  • Identify your estimand ATE (Average Treatment Effect): effect averaged over the whole population. ATT (Average Treatment Effect on the Treated): effect on those who actually received treatment. LATE (Local Average Treatment Effect): effect for compliers in an IV design. Know which you need before choosing a method.
  • Match your method to your data structure Randomised experiment (RCT): gold standard, directly estimates ATE. Difference-in-Differences: panel data with pre/post and treatment/control groups. Regression Discontinuity: assignment rule with a threshold. Instrumental Variables: when treatment is endogenous. Propensity Score Matching: observational data, no RCT.

Stage 02

Difference-in-Differences (DiD)

DiD is the standard technique for estimating the causal effect of a policy or programme using panel data — where you observe units before and after treatment, with a comparison group.

  • Set up the panel structure Each row = one unit × one time period. Columns: unit_id, period (pre/post), treated (1/0), outcome. Every unit must be observed in both periods.
  • Visualise the parallel trends assumption Plot the average outcome over time for treatment and control groups in the pre-treatment period. If both groups were trending in the same direction before treatment, the parallel trends assumption is plausible.Parallel trends is untestable in the post-period, but pre-period divergence is strong evidence against it. If pre-trends differ: DiD is invalid.
  • Estimate the DiD using regression Regress outcome on: treated (group indicator), post (time indicator), and treated × post (the interaction). The coefficient on the interaction term is the DiD estimate — the causal treatment effect.

Always show this plot explicitly to the audience — it is the credibility test for the causal claim.
Stage 03

A/B Test Analysis & Propensity Score Matching

Two further techniques: A/B test analysis (the cleanest case) and propensity score matching (when randomisation was not possible).

  • A/B test: regression with treatment indicator Regress outcome on treatment indicator (+ any pre-specified covariates for efficiency). The coefficient on the treatment indicator is the ATE. Report 95% CI and p-value.
  • Propensity score matching When randomisation was not possible, match each treated unit to a control unit with similar pre-treatment features (propensity score = P(treated | features)). The matched comparison removes confounding from observable variables. Always test balance after matching.
  • Report effect sizes, not just p-values A statistically significant effect that is economically tiny is not actionable. Always translate coefficients to business units (£, %, customers). A p-value of 0.001 on a +0.3pp margin effect may not justify the cost of a rollout.

Red flags
  • Regression coefficient interpreted as causal without experimental or quasi-experimental design

  • Propensity score matching used without post-matching balance check
  • Only p-value reported — no effect size
