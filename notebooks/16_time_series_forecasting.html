Time Series Forecasting

Question: What will this metric be next period?Output: Point forecast + intervalDataset: timeseries.csv (60 months)Use case: Revenue forecasting · Demand planning · Budget scenario modelling

Pipeline walkthrough — analyst workflow steps, pre-populated outputs, and business interpretation at each stage.

Stage 01

Time Series Problem Setup

Time series forecasting predicts future values of a metric using its own historical pattern. The critical distinction from cross-sectional regression: observations are ordered and correlated in time. Violating this assumption (e.g., random train/test split) produces falsely optimistic performance estimates.

  • Always use time-based train/test split Split at a point in time: everything before date X = train, everything after = test. Never use random split — this leaks future values into the training window.Random split on time series data typically inflates AUC/R2 by 10-20 points. Always date-boundary split.
  • Decompose the series first Before modelling, decompose into: trend (long-run direction), seasonality (repeating pattern), and residual (noise). This tells you which components a model must capture.
  • Establish a naive baseline Seasonal naive: predict next month = same month last year. This is surprisingly hard to beat. Any model must outperform this baseline to justify complexity.

Stage 02

Fit Multiple Models & Compare

Fit at least three models: a baseline, a statistical model (SARIMA or ETS), and an ML model (LightGBM with lag features). Compare on the held-out test period.

  • Seasonal naive baseline predict(month t) = actual(month t-12). No training required. Serves as the minimum acceptable performance bar.
  • ETS (Exponential Smoothing) Captures trend and seasonality automatically. Fits smoothing parameters by maximising likelihood. Generally outperforms naive on trending series.
  • LightGBM with lag features Create features: lag_1 (last month), lag_12 (same month last year), lag_3_avg (3-month rolling avg), month_of_year (1-12), quarter. Then treat as a standard regression problem. Captures complex patterns the statistical models miss.
  • SARIMA if data is stationary Seasonal ARIMA: explicit seasonal differencing and autoregressive structure. Good for stationary series with clear AR/MA components. Requires stationarity testing (ADF test).

What this tells you
  • LightGBM with lag features reduces forecast error by 44% vs seasonal naive baseline — a meaningful, business-significant improvement.
  • The ensemble (LightGBM + ETS average) achieves the best test performance. Ensembling two different model types is a reliable technique for reducing forecast variance.
  • MAPE of 3.4% means typical forecast error is 3.4% of the actual value — at a £400K monthly revenue run rate, that is ±£13,600 average error. Acceptable for planning purposes.
  • November and December peak months are the most important for business planning — both are within 3% of actual. The model handles seasonality well.
Forecast horizon matters — performance degrades with distanceMAPE of 3.4% on 1-month-ahead forecast will be higher (typically 6-10%) on 3-month-ahead and higher still on 6-month-ahead. Always report the forecast horizon alongside the performance metric. A model that looks excellent at 1-month may be unusable at 6-month.
Stage 03

Forecast Intervals & QA

Point forecasts without uncertainty intervals are incomplete. Decision-makers need to know the range of likely outcomes, not just the central estimate.

  • Generate prediction intervals For ETS/SARIMA: built-in confidence intervals. For LightGBM: quantile regression (train Q10 and Q90 models in addition to Q50). Report as: central forecast ± band.
  • Check for structural breaks If a major event (COVID, new product, market disruption) falls in the test period, model error will spike. Document known structural breaks and consider treating them separately.
  • Monitor for forecast drift As new actuals arrive, track whether the model is systematically over- or under-forecasting. A rolling bias of > 3 months signals the model needs retraining.

Red flags
