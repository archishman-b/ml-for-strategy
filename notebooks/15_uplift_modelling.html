Pipeline walkthrough — analyst workflow steps, pre-populated outputs, and business interpretation at each stage.

Stage 01

Why Uplift — Not Just Churn Propensity

A churn model tells you who is at risk. An uplift model tells you who will respond to an intervention. These are different questions — and the difference directly affects ROI. Targeting high-churn customers who would have stayed anyway wastes budget and may even accelerate churn.

  • Understand the four customer groups Every customer falls into one of four buckets: (1) Persuadables — at risk, intervention works. (2) Sure Things — would stay anyway, intervention wastes money. (3) Lost Causes — will churn regardless. (4) Sleeping Dogs — stable but intervention might disturb them. Only group 1 deserves targeting.A churn model alone cannot distinguish Persuadables from Sure Things. Only uplift modelling can.
  • Require a randomised experiment Uplift modelling requires historical data where some customers were contacted (treatment group) and others were not (control group) — ideally by random assignment. Without this, you cannot estimate counterfactual outcomes.
  • Set up the treatment indicator T=1: customer received intervention (retention call/offer). T=0: customer did not. Y=1: churned. Y=0: retained. Goal: estimate P(Y=0 | T=1) - P(Y=0 | T=0) per customer.

Stage 02

Train the Uplift Model

Two common approaches: the Two-Model approach (simple, interpretable) and the Class Transformation approach (single model, theoretically elegant). Start with Two-Model.

  • Two-Model approach Train two separate churn models: one on the treatment group, one on the control group. For each customer, compute: uplift = P(churn | T=1, features) - P(churn | T=0, features). Negative uplift = intervention reduces churn risk.Simple and explainable. Works well in practice despite the two-model approximation.
  • Score all customers with uplift estimate Apply both models to all customers. Subtract treatment model score from control model score. Sort customers by uplift (most negative = most persuadable).
  • Build the uplift decile table Sort customers by uplift score. Group into 10 equal buckets (deciles). For each decile, compute: average treatment effect (churn rate treatment minus churn rate control). The top decile should show the largest treatment effect.

What this tells you
  • Top decile: contacting these customers reduces churn by 32 percentage points. At 50 customers × £1,200 CLTV × 32% = £19,200 protected revenue per intervention cycle.
  • Bottom two deciles (uplift > 0): contacting these customers appears to increase churn by 4pp. These are Sleeping Dogs — satisfied customers who become aware of switching options when approached.
  • The uplift table validates the model: the actual treatment effect decreases monotonically from decile 1 to 10 — the model is correctly ranking customers by persuadability.
Uplift ROI is 3-5x higher than churn targetingA churn model targeting the top 20% by risk score might include 50% Sure Things (would have stayed anyway). An uplift model targeting the top 20% by persuadability score reduces that waste significantly. The net ROI improvement is typically 2-5x on retention spend — the most directly business-impactful ML technique in customer retention.
Stage 03

QA

Critical checks before deploying uplift scores for campaign targeting.

  • Validate randomisation Pre-treatment features must be balanced between treatment and control. Any imbalance = biased uplift estimates.
  • Check uplift decile monotonicity The actual treatment effect should decrease as you go down the decile table. Non-monotonic pattern = model is not reliably ranking by persuadability.
  • Confirm Sleeping Dog group exists If no customers have positive uplift, the model may be miscalibrated or the intervention has no negative side effects — unusual and worth questioning.
Output: QA Checklist Checklist
Good
  • Randomised experiment (or quasi-experimental design documented)
  • Pre-treatment feature balance verified
  • Uplift deciles show monotonic treatment effect
  • Sleeping Dog group identified and excluded from targeting list
  • ROI calculation: uplift × CLTV × conversion rate vs cost of intervention
Red flags
  • Observational data used without adjustment (selection bias)
  • Only overall ATE reported — no individual-level scores
  • Top decile by churn propensity used instead of uplift score
  • Sleeping Dogs contacted (may increase churn)
  • Uplift validated only on training set, not held-out test set
